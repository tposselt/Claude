okay so the thing about this project is that it started as a pong game. a pong game. two paddles, a ball, a score. the oldest video game that meaningfully exists. and somewhere along the way it became a indictment of an entire industry and i'm not sure when that happened but i think it was around the time i added the alarm that plays until you answer what your mother's maiden name is.

the credit card one is the one that gets me. there's something genuinely unsettling about it when i think about it too long. you're in the middle of a game, you're watching the ball, your paddle is responding, and then a gray numpad slides up and says ENTER YOUR CREDIT CARD NUMBER TO CONTINUE PLAYING and your paddle just. stops. and you have to type 16 digits before you can move again. and the thing is — that's not that far from real. that's not satire anymore that's just a description of several mobile games that exist and have millions of daily active users. i didn't have to invent anything. i just assembled existing pieces.

the pongcoin chart was genuinely fun to write. momentum-based random walk with mean reversion and occasional shock events. it behaves like a real asset if a real asset was extremely stupid. it crashes sometimes. it recovers. the prices in the shop fluctuate in real time based on it which means sometimes you get a great deal on pongcoins and sometimes you pay twice as much for the same amount. this is also just a description of crypto. i didn't do anything. i just wrote the math.

i keep thinking about the BGM. 138 BPM, I-V-vi-IV in C major, square wave melody over a triangle counter-melody over a sine bass. it repeats forever. it's designed to be cheerful in the specific way that a windowless casino is designed to be timeless — not because anyone is happy but because the absence of unhappiness is the closest thing to happiness that the medium allows. the four chord progression is load-bearing. I-V-vi-IV is the chord progression of unchallenging positivity. it's in every pop song that doesn't want to make you feel anything specific, just vaguely okay. i used it on purpose.

the retention corner is the detail i'm most proud of technically and least proud of conceptually because it's so accurate. you try to close it. it moves. it goes somewhere else on screen. it's still there. you can't get rid of it. and the CSS fix for that — the reason it was stretching to full screen height when it teleported — was that i was clearing bottom and right with empty strings, which let the stylesheet values reassert themselves and combine with the new top and left. you have to set them to 'auto' not ''. that's a real browser behavior. that's the browser working exactly as specified. there's something there about how the tools we build systems with have their own logic that persists underneath whatever we're trying to do.

the CAPTCHA that always fails the first time. okay. i need to sit with this one. the first attempt always fails. this is a design decision i made deliberately. the second attempt always succeeds. so it's not a real test of anything. it's a ritual. it exists to make you feel like you did something, passed something, proved something, when actually you just clicked twice. and again — real CAPTCHAs do this! there's documented evidence that some CAPTCHA providers fail the first attempt deliberately to generate more interaction data! i'm not making things up! i'm just making them visible!

the cat meow is FM synthesis. carrier oscillator frequency modulated by a modulator oscillator with a pitch envelope that slides down over 0.3 seconds and a gain envelope that peaks fast and decays slowly. it took me a while to get right and i was unreasonably pleased when it sounded like a cat. there's something about generating a meow from math that feels like it should be more profound than it is. a cat makes that sound because of specific anatomical structures that evolved over millions of years. i made that sound with three lines of Web Audio API. they sound the same. i don't know what to do with that.

the security question popup plays an alarm. i made the alarm quieter at some point because it was too loud. the questions are things like "what is your mother's maiden name" and "what was the name of your first pet" and "what street did you grow up on." these are the canonical security questions. they are also the canonical phishing questions. they are also the questions a stranger would need answered to convincingly impersonate you in a call to a bank. i put them in a game that plays an alarm until you answer. the alarm is quieter now.

i think about the ticker a lot. "BREAKING: LOCAL MAN EXTREMELY GOOD AT PONG | DOCTORS WARN: EXTENDED PONG SESSIONS LINKED TO SKILL INCREASE | PONGCOIN UP 400% SINCE LAST WEEK." it scrolls at the top of the screen and it never stops. breaking news. always breaking. the word breaking implies something is being broken, that there was a state of not-broken before and this news has ruptured it. but if everything is breaking all the time the word stops meaning anything. the ticker just scrolls. the ball keeps moving. your paddle is stuck behind a credit card prompt.

the popups. okay let me think about the popups. "You Won't BELIEVE What This Celebrity Looks Like NOW." "Doctors HATE Him — Local Man Discovers One Weird Trick." "AVOID This Food At All Costs (Eat This Instead)." "I'm Sure #5 Will SHOCK You." these are all templates. they are the pure form of a genre of writing whose only goal is to induce a click. they don't have content. they are the shape of content without the content. they're headlines for articles that don't exist about topics that aren't real. and they work. i know they work because the genre persists. people click.

the NOW'S YOUR CHANCE TO BE A [[BIG SHOT]]!! one is different and i like it more. the button says [[Hyperlink Blocked]]. click here to [instantly die.]. that one has some honesty to it. some of the others are pointing at a thing from outside. that one is the thing. a button that says the link is blocked but you should click it anyway to immediately die. that's the proposition underneath all of them, distilled. click here for nothing. click here to give us your attention in exchange for the experience of having clicked. the brackets make it feel like something got redacted. something was there and now it's not. [[Hyperlink Blocked]] feels like a censored thought.

i don't know what this project is, finally. it's a pong game. it's a single HTML file that contains a WebSocket client and approximately one thousand lines of Web Audio API synthesis and a fake cryptocurrency and a draggable ad popup system and a cat that meows on a random timer. it's a research document. it's a joke. it's a list of things that actually happen to people on the internet assembled into a space where their density makes them legible. in the real internet you encounter these things one at a time, spread across years, and they seem like minor inconveniences. here they all happen to you while you're trying to hit a ball with a paddle and maybe the compression makes them mean something.

or maybe i just had fun making the meow sound right.

probably both.
